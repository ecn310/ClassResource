Section 9 Statistics

9.1 Descriptive Statistics

Learning objectives (Daniels & Minot, pp. 75-80, 81-90, 96-101):

  1. List the uses for descriptive statistics in research.
  2. Distinguish between ordinal categorical variables, nominal categorical variables, continuous variables on an interval scale, and continuous variables on a ratio scale.
  3. Include the number of missing values in a frequency table and think critically about which values are missing.
  4. Make appropriate use of bar graphs and histograms.
  5. Analyze data (ordinal, interval scale, ratio scale) using the median and percentiles of a variable.
  6. Analyze continuous data using mean, variance, standard deviation, and coefficient of variation.

9.1.1 Median

  • Median is 50th percentile
    • Equivalent: rank a variable from lowest to highest and identify the observation that falls exactly in the middle.
  • A MEAN will be skewed in one direction if there are a small number of unusually high or low values

9.1.2 Variance

  • is a measure of how spread out the values of one variable are from their mean.
  • is a measure of how far each value falls from the mean.
  • is the square of the standard deviation.
  • is zero when all the values are the same.

9.1.3 Descriptive Statistics exercise

  • You will submit individually, although you can ask others for help.
  • Choose one variable of interest in your project’s data
    • Everyone on your team must use a different variable
    • Explore the variable using tools we’ve learned from D&M
  • Share something you learn about the variable on the “Descriptive statistics exercise” discussion (example in first comment)
    • Say what dataset you’re using and where it comes from
    • Explain what the variable means
    • Share one APPROPRIATE figure (e.g. histogram or bar chart)
      • Calculate one related descriptive statistic (mean, median, standard deviation, frequency, etc)
    • Explain what you’ve learned through the combination of your figure and descriptive statistic
      • They should NOT be two unrelated facts
      • How might this information impact your choices when doing analysis? What do you learn that makes you think about your data differently, or what questions does it raise?

Formatting Guidelines

  • Put everything in narrative form (not bullet points) like in a report
    • it’s meant to tell a story, not be presented as Q&A. The list of questions is there to guide you. Part of what I want you to practice is how to weave facts together into a story.
  • No screenshots
    • Especially for descriptive stats—use the stats in a sentence
    • We’re using this to start to move toward how we would write in the report, and we would never put a screenshot in a report.
  • Consider size and centering of your graph
  • The standard way of talking about skewness is really counterintuitive; I suggest saying that it’s skewed, then where most of the observations are (or where few of the observations are), not “skewed toward“
  • Your narrative should say something definitive about scale and definition of your variable; you may have to dig into a codebook
  • To talk about variance, you have to contextualize with the mean–variance/standard deviation can’t be interpreted on its own

9.2 Null Hypothesis

Daniels & Minot, pp. 121-122, 124-125 (Section 7.4, 7.6, 7.7) Learning objectives:

  1. Define the term null hypothesis and formulate a null hypothesis related to your research question.
  2. Use p-values (the book calls this the “alpha level”) to determine when the null hypothesis can be rejected.

The alpha level (or p-value)

  • is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true.
  • is the probability of a Type 1 error.
  • is typically set at 0.05.

We DO NOT say that we “accept” the null hypothesis when the p-value is below 0.05. This is because samples do not always accurately reflect the population.

9.2.1 Null hypotheses for projects

  • Tariffs: Domestic taxes will increase in developed countries that limit tariffs. This relationship will be stronger in countries with high import-to-GDP ratios than in countries with low import-to-GDP ratios. (?)
  • Supply Chain: Need to find

9.3 One-Sample t Test

Daniels & Minot, pp. 133-135, 137-139 (Section 8.2, 8.4, 8.5) Learning objectives:

  1. Determine when it is appropriate to use the one-sample t test.
  2. Conduct one-sample t tests in Stata and interpret the results.

9.3.1 One sample t-test demo (Stata)

* Decide how many draws of data we want
set obs 1000
* Generate data from normal distribution with mean = 1,
standard deviation = 0.1
gen X = rnormal(1, 0.1)
* Test whether mean of X is equal to 0, or to 1
ttest X == 0
ttest X == 1
* Repeat above but for larger standard deviation
gen Y = rnormal(1, 1)
ttest Y == 0
ttest Y == 1
